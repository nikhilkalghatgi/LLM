### **SnoopyScone** *( 1y ago )*

Okay, I understand where you’re coming from. Let me give you a detailed explanation. While your argument highlights the ‘impressive’ generalization capabilities of LLMs, it overlooks a variety of critical aspects of ML/DL that make them relevant:

Efficiency and specialization matters. Smaller, task-specific models require way less computational power to achieve superior performance compared to LLMs.

LLMs struggle with certain domains. They still face limitations in fields requiring real-time decision making, precise numerical computation, or domain-specific optimization. Like another user said, tasks like protein structure prediction, reinforcement learning for robotics, and computer vision require models with different architectures and cannot be solved by token generators.

Training on multiple tasks doesn’t always lead to better performance. I should have put this at the top, perhaps. Your example about training on multiple games improving Minecraft performance is intriguing for sure (I’ll look into it), but not universally applicable. In many real-world cases, multi-task training leads to negative transfer, where knowledge from one task actively degrades performance on another.

The goal shouldn’t be to replace ML/DL models with LLMs, but rather integrate LLMs with ML/DL models. Agentic AI systems use LLMs as reasoning engines while leveraging specialized ML/DL models for execution. For example, GPT-4 Turbo can reason about an image, but for complex image segmentation, it’s outperformed by your average Joe’s CNNs.

LLMs DONOT solve ML’s core research problems. Do you think LLMs can come up with newer architectures that it hasn’t seen while training? Deep Learning era is not over. It won’t be over for a long time. While agentic AI is exciting, you cannot dismiss fundamental ML/DL. For the moment, LLMs can and should be used only as a reasoning agent WITH ML/DL, and not for replacing them.
